Hi. My last post about COLTS was, in retrospect, quite inflammatory and unsubstantiated, and I apologize to anyone I upset with it. I hate drama, I really do, but that is often what sucks people down the rabbit hole. So, let's avoid pointless shit flinging and talking past each other this time, yeah? We're all here to mod, and we all want to make interesting features on the versions our players and ourselves enjoy modding and playing on, so please, shut the fuck up about The One True Version.

## What is a COLTS?
Now that we have that out of the way, *what is COLTS?* COLTS or COmmunity LTS (formerly known as SMV or Standard Modding Version) was a misguided attempt to standardize *the* modding version. Of course, [the road to drama is paved with well meaning modders](https://quiltmc.org/), so you likely won't be surprised to hear that it caused an uproar among smaller, more shut off modding communities, many of which were predominantly latest-version modders or even older modders.

*But what's so controversial about that?* That's a reasonable question, but reasonable answers in Minecraft modding are often sparse in comparison. Essentially, the original phrasing of the goals of COLTS (formerly SMV) were more insidious, and those involved were stirring the pot quite heavily (**insert proof and go over it, I don't want to be speaking out of my ass here**). This only widened the divide between the entirely artificial two sides and fueled further conspiracy theories that COLTS advocates secretly wanted to kill newer versions of Minecraft, treating them more like a secretive organization (**maybe more proof**). I honestly don't understand what compels these people to come up with these ideas, but this shit needs to stop. Seriously.

## Considerations
An effective Community LTS (COLTS) project should be focused more on description rather than prescription. People want to mod and play their mods on whatever version they want to, and they will, so what use does an overgeneralized standard version serve? It would be nice if there were a reliable source you could reference to see what versions certain genres of mods and modpacks are predominantly found. So, that's exactly what we should do: categorized version concentration, or in other words, mods of genre go in, versions of genre come out. Of course, we'll want to show more useful data like how many mods in general are in what version, how many mods/modpacks in a genre are in each version, and how willing mod developers and modpackers of a certain genre and in general are to develop for certain versions.
Strangely, I haven't seen anyone else with this idea, let alone want to follow through with it.
